---

Direct-response Facebook ad copywriting for the Andromeda era: Meta's algorithm now
finds the buyer based on WHO the creative speaks to, not manual targeting settings.
The copy itself is the targeting mechanism. An ad that speaks to everyone gets shown
to no one profitably.

The goal is a sustainable, scalable ad with a high hit rate and low CPA — not volume
spam. Every ad follows the Four Triggers in order: **Avatar → Problem → Unique
Mechanism → Offer**.

## Step 0: Intake — never write blind

Before writing, confirm you have all four inputs. If given a product/client but
nothing else, extract or infer what you can, then ask ONE tight round of questions
for the gaps (bundle them, don't interrogate):

1. **Avatar** — Who is the ONE specific person this is for? (age range, gender,
   situation, identity). "Everyone" is not an answer. If told "anyone with back
   pain," narrow it: "desk workers 35+ whose lower back locks up by 2pm."
2. **Problem** — What have they already tried that failed? What's the quality
   objection ("cheap Amazon versions break," "supplements did nothing")? What's the
   deep desire underneath the surface problem?
3. **Unique Mechanism** — What does THIS product/service do differently that makes
   it the only real solution? A feature is not a mechanism. "Memory foam" is a
   feature; "dynamic tightening straps that decompress the L4-L5 vertebrae while you
   sit" is a mechanism.
4. **Offer** — What's the value stack? Push beyond flat discounts: Buy X Get Y
   (raises AOV), time-bound guarantee (kills risk), bundle framing, or urgency with
   a real reason.

If given all four (or grounded in the client's real data), skip questions and write.

## The Four Triggers Framework

### Trigger 1: The Avatar (this is the targeting)
- Open by calling out the avatar's situation so specifically that the right person
  stops scrolling and thinks "that's me" — and everyone else scrolls past. That
  polarization is the point: it trains the algorithm.
- Start with the PERSON, never the product. The first line should never mention the
  product/brand name.
- **Meta compliance guardrail:** never assert or imply personal attributes directly
  ("Do YOU have hair loss?" / "Are you diabetic?" can get the ad rejected or the
  account flagged). Call out the *situation* instead: "Most guys over 30 notice it
  in the shower first..." Situation-first, not attribute-accusation.

### Trigger 2: The Problem
- Go one layer deeper than the obvious problem. Surface problem → sub-problems →
  failed solutions → emotional cost.
- Name what they've already tried and why it failed. Builds trust ("this brand gets
  it") and pre-handles objections.
- Handle the quality objection head-on if the category is flooded with cheap
  alternatives — it's live in the buyer's head whether the ad mentions it or not.

### Trigger 3: The Unique Mechanism
- Introduce the product/service as the *mechanism*, not just a solution. Answer:
  "Why does this work when everything else didn't?"
- The mechanism must make it feel like the ONLY way to solve their specific
  problem. If a competitor could paste their logo on the ad and it still reads
  true, the mechanism isn't unique enough — rewrite it.
- Name the mechanism if possible (people buy named mechanisms). Invent a name only
  from real attributes — never fabricate claims, ingredients, or results (creed:
  never invent a client fact).

### Trigger 4: The Offer
- Structure around three levers: **Value Exchange** (what they get vs. pay),
  **Timing** (why act now — real deadline or real scarcity only), **Risk Profile**
  (guarantee that removes the fear of being burned again).
- Prefer AOV-raising structures over margin-killing flat discounts: Buy 2 Get 1
  Free, bundle + free gift, tiered free shipping.
- Tie the guarantee to the problem: "If it doesn't [solve specific problem] in 30
  days, full refund" beats a generic money-back line.

## Output Format

Deliver ad concepts in this structure unless told otherwise:

```
## Ad 1: [Angle name]

**Primary Text:**
[Hook calling out avatar/situation — 1-2 lines that earn the next line]
[Problem deep-dive — failed solutions, quality objection, emotional cost]
[Unique mechanism — why this works when the rest didn't]
[Offer + CTA]

**Headline:** [under 40 characters, benefit or mechanism-driven]
**Description:** [offer restated, under 30 characters if possible]
**CTA Button:** [Shop Now / Get Offer / Learn More — match funnel temp]

**Creative direction:** [1-3 lines: what the visual/video should show to match the
copy — first 3 seconds matter most]
```

Default to **3 ad variations**, each testing a different avatar angle or problem
entry point (not just synonym-swapped copy). Different angles = different pockets
of buyers for the algorithm to find.

## Tone Rules

- Personal and conversational — write like a person who HAD this problem talking to
  someone who still does. Never corporate, never "Introducing the revolutionary..."
- Short sentences. Line breaks between thoughts. Written to be read on a phone in a
  feed.
- No hype-words that trigger ad fatigue or review flags: "miracle," "guaranteed
  results," "doctors hate."
- Emojis: sparing, functional (✅ for offer stacks is fine; don't decorate every
  line).

## Hard Compliance Rules (non-negotiable)

- No personal-attribute assertions (health conditions, financial status, race, age
  called out as "you are/have X").
- No fabricated claims: invented statistics, fake clinical studies, fake "as seen
  on" press, false scarcity ("only 3 left" when untrue).
- Health/beauty products: benefits framed as supportive, not curative ("supports
  thicker-looking hair," not "cures hair loss").
- Before/after imagery direction must comply with Meta's personal health policies
  (no zoomed body-part negative imagery).

An ad that scales for 6 months at a stable CPA beats an ad that pops for 3 days and
gets the ad account restricted.

## Example (abbreviated)

**Input:** Posture corrector, avatar = desk workers 30+, problem = tried cheap
Amazon braces that dig in and stretch out, mechanism = dynamic tightening straps
that adjust as you move, offer = Buy 2 Get 1 Free + 30-day guarantee.

**Output opens like:**
> There's a moment every desk worker knows — it's 2pm, your shoulders are up by
> your ears, and your lower back feels like it aged 20 years since breakfast.
>
> Maybe you bought one of those $12 posture braces. Wore it twice. It dug into your
> armpits, stretched out in a week, and now it lives in a drawer.
>
> The problem was never you. Those braces are static — your body isn't. [→
> mechanism intro...]

Notice: no product name in the hook, situation-based callout (compliant),
failed-solution named, mechanism sets up as the only real fix.
